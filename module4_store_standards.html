Floors
Vacuuming & mopping — what's required
›

Floors are the first thing customers look at when they walk in — especially in a retail environment with glass display cases. Once daily is the minimum. More frequently if it's busy, wet outside, or visibly needed.

✓
Vacuum the entire floor area — including behind counters and along the base of display cases where dust accumulates.
At minimum once per day. Do this before mopping — vacuuming first removes loose debris so the mop doesn't just push it around.
Daily
✓
Mop all hard floor surfaces using the correct solution. Don't leave the floor visibly streaky or wet.
At minimum once per day, after vacuuming. Put out a wet floor sign while mopping and leave it until the floor is dry.
Daily
✓
Spot-clean between cycles — if something spills or the floor gets visibly dirty during a shift, deal with it immediately. Don't wait for the scheduled clean.
A wet floor or spill left unattended is also a safety hazard.
As needed
💡Timing tip: Vacuuming and mopping are best done during a quiet period or at the start/end of a shift — not when the store is busy with customers. Plan around foot traffic.
Glass & surfaces
Display cases, counters & windows
›

Glass display cases and counters are where customers spend most of their time. Fingerprints, smudges, and dust on glass surfaces make the store look neglected — and make products harder to see clearly.

✓
Wipe down all glass display case surfaces — inside and out. Use a glass cleaner and a lint-free cloth. No streaks.
Check from the customer's side — that's what they see. Not just from behind the counter.
Every shift
✓
Clean the service counter surface — wipe down after each transaction if needed, and thoroughly at the start and end of each shift.
The counter is where the customer's hands are. It should always look clean.
Every shift
✓
Clean storefront windows and door glass — fingerprints on the front door or windows are visible from outside and affect first impressions before the customer even enters.
Daily
✓
Dust display cabinet tops and shelf edges — dust builds up quickly around products and on the top surfaces of cabinets. Use a microfibre cloth.
Every shift
⚠️Don't use paper towel on glass — it leaves lint. Use a proper glass cloth or microfibre. And always spray the cloth, not the glass directly, when cleaning near products.
Bathrooms
Bathroom standards & checks
›

If your store has a customer-accessible bathroom, it reflects directly on the store. A dirty bathroom tells customers you don't care about the details — and they'll extend that assumption to your products and service.

✓
Check the bathroom at the start of every shift — clean toilet, sink, mirror, and floor. Restock toilet paper and hand soap if needed.
Every shift
✓
Do a mid-shift check during busy periods — if the store is busy or the bathroom has had a lot of use, check it again mid-shift.
As needed
✓
Clean the toilet, sink, and mirror — use the appropriate cleaning products. The mirror and tap fixtures should be streak-free. The toilet bowl and seat should be visibly clean.
Daily
✓
Empty the bin before it overflows — a full or overflowing bin is one of the most common bathroom complaints. Don't wait until it's a problem.
As needed
ℹ️If you find the bathroom in a state that requires more than routine cleaning, flag it to your manager rather than ignoring it. Don't leave it for the next person.

Product presentation

How your shelves look influences what customers buy — and whether they trust the store. Tidy, well-faced shelves signal professionalism and make it easy for customers to find what they need.

Facing
Facing products — what it means and how to do it
›

Facing means pulling products to the front edge of the shelf with their labels forward-facing, so every shelf looks full and organised. It takes a few minutes but makes the entire store look significantly more professional.

✓
Pull all products to the front of the shelf — no gaps at the front edge. If stock is low, consolidate to one side rather than leaving scattered items across a shelf.
Every shift
✓
Labels should be forward-facing — every bottle, box, and device should have its label or front panel visible to the customer. No products turned sideways or backwards.
Every shift
✓
Group products consistently — juice by brand or flavour category, devices by type. Don't let stock get mixed up when restocking. Consistency helps customers find things faster and makes upselling easier.
Every shift
✓
Remove any damaged, dusty, or price-stickered-over packaging — a product that looks like it's been on the shelf for years doesn't inspire confidence. Flag old or damaged stock to your manager.
As needed
✓
Check display cases for fingerprints after facing — handling products to face them often leaves prints on the glass. Wipe down after.
After facing
💡A well-faced shelf is quicker to restock and easier to manage. Get into the habit of facing as you go — a quick tidy during quiet moments keeps the store looking sharp all shift without a big effort at the end.
Display cases
Keeping display cases looking their best
›

Display cases are the centrepiece of the store. They need to look clean and deliberate at all times — not like a junk drawer.

What to checkStandard
Product spacingItems should be evenly spaced — not crammed together or scattered. Leave enough room that each product can be seen clearly.
OrientationDevices should be upright or positioned as intended. No items on their sides or stacked carelessly.
Price tagsEvery item should have a visible, legible price. Missing or peeling price tags should be replaced immediately.
LightingIf the case has internal lighting, make sure it's working. Report any burnt-out bulbs to your manager.
Glass cleanlinessCheck from the customer's vantage point — not from behind the counter. Fingerprints on customer-side glass are easy to miss from your angle.
No personal items insideNothing that doesn't belong — no personal items, food wrappers, charging cables, or paperwork inside or on top of display cases.
Restocking
Restocking shelves the right way
›

Restocking isn't just filling shelves — it's maintaining the store's presentation. Done carelessly, it undoes everything that facing accomplished.

✓ Do this
Rotate stock — older product at the front, new stock behind.
Face as you restock — don't leave it for later.
Keep like products together in the same section.
Check for any expired or damaged stock as you go.
Wipe the shelf before restocking if there's visible dust.
✗ Don't do this
Dump new stock at the front without rotating.
Leave half-empty boxes or packaging on the shelf.
Mix products from different categories in the same space.
Leave a shelf partially restocked and "come back to it."
Restock while serving customers — finish the transaction first.
Professional conduct

How you present yourself on the floor is as important as how the store looks. Customers form opinions about the business based on what they see when they walk in — and that includes you.

Phone use
Personal phone use on the floor
›

A customer should never walk into the store and see a staff member on their phone. It communicates immediately that the staff member doesn't care — and by extension, that the store doesn't care. It's one of the most common and avoidable first impressions that loses customers.

✗
No personal phone use on the sales floor — not at the counter, not in the display case area, not where customers can see you. If you need to use your phone, step to the back.
This includes texting, scrolling, watching videos, and taking calls.
✗
Never be on your phone when a customer enters — the moment the door opens, your attention should be on the customer. If you're on your phone and a customer walks in, the damage is already done. They've seen it.
✓
Phone use is fine on breaks in the break area — this isn't about your breaks. It's about what the customer sees on the floor.
✓
If you're using the store's point-of-sale or a work device, that's different — just be mindful that it looks like work, not personal use.
⚠️If it's a genuinely urgent personal call or message, step to the back and deal with it quickly. A brief absence is far better than a customer watching you scroll.
Belongings
Personal belongings on the counter and sales floor
›

The service counter and sales floor are for the customer — not for your stuff. Personal items left in the customer area make the space look unprofessional and cluttered, and they signal to customers that this is someone's hangout space rather than a retail environment.

✗
No bags, backpacks, or jackets on or behind the counter — store personal items in the back or in a designated staff area. Not draped over the counter chair or sitting on the floor behind the till.
✗
No food or drinks on the counter or sales floor — unless it's a closed water bottle kept out of sight. Food packaging, takeaway cups, and snacks on the counter look unprofessional and can be off-putting to customers.
✗
No personal items on display shelves or in display cases — keys, earphones, receipts, personal vapes — none of it belongs in the customer-facing display space.
✗
No paperwork or clutter visible to customers on the counter — if you need to have notes or paperwork out, keep it below the counter line or put it away when customers are present.
💡A quick rule of thumb: if it doesn't belong to the store and it's in the customer area, it shouldn't be there. Walk the floor with fresh eyes at the start of your shift and remove anything that doesn't belong.
Floor presence
How to carry yourself on the sales floor
›

During quiet periods, it can be tempting to sit down, lean on the counter, or look bored. Customers notice all of it. Looking engaged and attentive — even when no one is in the store — is part of the job.

SituationRight approach
Quiet period with no customersUse the time productively — face shelves, clean glass, tidy stock, or do other tasks. Standing attentively is far better than visibly killing time.
Customer enters the storeAcknowledge them immediately with a greeting. If you're mid-task, pause it, make eye contact, and greet them before continuing.
Multiple customers in storeAcknowledge everyone — a brief nod or "I'll be with you in a moment" to customers waiting keeps them from feeling ignored.
Mid-shift fatigueStay aware of how you're coming across. Slouching, sighing, or visibly looking at the clock reads as disengaged. If you need a reset, take your break.
Personal conversations with colleaguesKeep them brief and professional on the floor. A customer walking into a staff chat can feel like they're interrupting — the conversation should stop when they enter.
ℹ️The store's appearance is a team effort — but it starts with individuals. If you see something that needs doing, do it without being asked. That attitude is what separates good staff from great ones.
