The sales approach

Good sales technique at The Vape Store isn't about pressure — it's about asking the right questions, listening carefully, and matching people to the right product. The best salespeople feel like knowledgeable friends.

👋
Greet every customer
Acknowledge every person who walks in. A simple "Hey, welcome in — let me know if you need anything" sets the tone without pressure.
🎧
Ask, then listen
Find out what they need before pitching anything. Two or three questions will tell you everything you need to make a great recommendation.
🎯
Match, don't push
Recommend what genuinely fits their situation. Customers who trust your advice come back. Customers who felt pressured don't.
🔁
Build the relationship
Repeat customers are the business. Every interaction is an investment. Remember preferences, follow up on how a product worked out, make them feel known.
Sales flow
GQRC: Greet, Qualify, Recommend, Close
›

Every successful sale at The Vape Store follows this four-step pattern. It takes about two minutes to learn and a few weeks to make feel natural. The faster you internalise it, the smoother your shifts become.

👋
Greet — make them feel welcome, not watched
Don't hover. Acknowledge them immediately, then give them space to look. Something like: "Hey, welcome in — feel free to have a look around, I'm just here if you need anything." If they look like they want help, approach. If they're browsing, wait for the signal.
🎧
Qualify — ask before you recommend
The three questions that tell you everything: (1) Are you new to vaping, or do you already have a device? (2) Are you trying to quit cigarettes or already switched? (3) What are you looking for today — a device, juice, or coils? Their answers dictate everything that follows. Don't skip this step.
🎯
Recommend — one clear suggestion, not a menu
Based on their answers, recommend one specific product — not three or four options. Giving someone who just walked in a choice between 12 devices causes decision paralysis and lost sales. Lead with your best-fit recommendation, then offer an alternative if they seem unsure. Use language like: "For someone in your situation, I'd point you toward this one — here's why."
✓
Close — move toward the transaction naturally
Don't be afraid to move toward the sale. Once they seem interested, a natural close sounds like: "Want me to grab one of those for you?" or "I'll pop a 3-pack of coils in with that — it's worth having them on hand." Keep it conversational. Never use pressure language like "this is the last one" unless it genuinely is.
💡Common mistake: Jumping straight to recommend without qualifying first. You'll end up pitching the wrong product, losing the customer's confidence, and having to backtrack. The qualifying step takes 30 seconds and saves minutes.

Language
What to say — and what to avoid
›

The words you use matter as much as what you're recommending. Small language habits make a big difference in how confident and trustworthy you come across.

✓ Say this
"For someone in your situation, I'd go with this one."
"Most people switching from cigarettes start here."
"It's worth grabbing a spare pack of coils at the same time."
"Let me know how you get on with it — you can always come back."
"That's a great choice — want me to grab it?"
✗ Avoid this
"I don't know, they're all pretty good honestly."
"It depends" (without following up with an actual answer).
"You should probably also get..." (sounds like a hard upsell).
"That's our most expensive option but it's the best."
"Honestly I've never tried that one myself."
⚠️Health claims: Never tell a customer that vaping is safe, healthy, or a guaranteed quit method. You can say it's an alternative to cigarettes and that many people use it to switch — that's it. Any stronger claim creates liability.
Recommending & upselling

The best upsell is one the customer doesn't notice as a upsell — because it's genuinely useful. Your goal is to send every customer home with everything they actually need.

Upselling
Natural upsell moments — and how to use them
›

An upsell isn't a trick — it's identifying when a better or additional product will genuinely improve the customer's experience. Upsells feel natural when they're relevant to what the customer just told you.

What the customer says/doesNatural upsellScript
Buying a disposable for the third timeSuggest an open pod system"You'd actually save quite a bit if you made the switch to a pod device — the upfront cost is more but the ongoing cost is much lower. Want me to show you a good starting option?"
Buying a single bottle of juiceSuggest a second flavour"That one's really popular — if you want something to rotate with it, this one pairs well. A lot of people grab two so they're not vaping the same thing all day."
Buying a refillable deviceCoil pack + juice bundle"I'll grab you a pack of coils to go with that — they'll need replacing after a couple of weeks. And have you picked a juice yet?"
Asking about battery lifeSpare batteries or a higher-capacity device"If battery life is a priority, it might be worth looking at this one — it's got a bigger battery and charges faster."
Buying a mod without a chargerExternal battery charger"Do you have a charger for 18650 batteries already? If not, I'd grab one — it's a lot safer than charging them in the mod."
⚠️One upsell per transaction. Stacking multiple add-ons feels pushy and damages trust. Pick the one that makes the most sense and focus there. If they say no, let it go gracefully.

Handling objections

An objection isn't a rejection — it's a question in disguise. Most customers who push back are still interested. Your job is to understand what's really behind the hesitation and address it calmly.

Framework
The ACE method — Acknowledge, Clarify, Explain
›

When a customer pushes back, don't argue and don't immediately capitulate. Use ACE: Acknowledge what they've said, Clarify what's really behind it, then Explain how the product or situation addresses that concern.

A
Acknowledge — validate their concern first
Never immediately disagree or dismiss. Start by acknowledging that their concern is reasonable: "That's a fair point" / "I totally understand why you'd think that" / "Yeah, I hear that a lot actually." This keeps the conversation open.
C
Clarify — understand what's really going on
Sometimes the stated objection isn't the real one. "It's too expensive" might actually mean "I'm not sure it'll work for me." Ask a follow-up: "Is it more the price, or are you not sure it's the right fit?" Knowing what you're actually dealing with lets you respond to the real concern.
E
Explain — address the concern directly and confidently
Give a clear, honest answer. Don't over-promise. If the concern is price, show the long-term value. If it's uncertainty, offer to start with something smaller-commitment. If you don't know the answer, say you'll find out rather than guessing.
💡If you've gone through ACE and the customer still isn't ready, respect that. "No problem at all — take your time and come back when you're ready" is a better close than a sale made under pressure that results in a return.
Common objections
Objections you'll hear every week — and how to handle them
›
ObjectionWhat's behind itHow to respond
"It's too expensive."Price sensitivity or value uncertaintyShow the cost-per-puff comparison vs cigarettes or disposables. Offer a more accessible entry point without dismissing the premium option entirely.
"I don't know if I'll stick with it."Commitment anxiety — common with switchersSuggest starting with something low-commitment: a disposable to test the experience, or the most affordable pod kit. "You don't have to go all in on day one."
"I tried vaping before and it didn't work."Bad previous experience — wrong device or nic levelAsk what they used. Most of the time they had the wrong setup. "A lot of people have that experience — they often had the wrong nicotine strength or device type. Let's figure out what actually suits you."
"My friend said this brand is bad."Social proof concernAcknowledge it: "I hear that sometimes — tastes and experiences vary a lot. What matters is matching the right product to how you vape." Redirect to their needs, not the brand debate.
"I'll just think about it."Not enough confidence in the recommendation, or they want to leave without saying noDon't push. Ask one gentle clarifying question: "Is there anything I can help clarify before you go?" If they're still leaving, let them go warmly — a comfortable exit keeps the door open.
"Can you do anything on the price?"Wants to feel like they got a dealBe honest about what you can and can't do. If you can't discount, offer genuine value: "I can't move on the price, but I'll make sure you leave with everything you need to get the most out of it."
Complaints
Handling complaints & returns
›

A customer who complains and gets it resolved well often becomes more loyal than one who never had an issue. How you handle it matters more than the complaint itself.

RuleWhat it means in practice
Listen fully before respondingDon't jump to solutions before the customer has finished explaining. Interrupting or deflecting too quickly signals defensiveness.
Never blame the customerEven if they clearly misused the product, focus on finding a solution rather than establishing fault. "Let's see what we can do" beats "well, you should have..."
Know the store's return policyAsk your manager to walk you through this before your first solo shift. Nothing's worse than making promises you can't keep.
Escalate without hesitationIf you're not sure, get a manager. Customers respect "let me get someone who can sort this properly" over a wrong answer delivered confidently.
Burnt taste complaintsAlmost always a coil issue — not primed properly, or past end of life. Diagnose before offering a replacement.
Device not workingCheck charge first. Then connection between pod and device. Then coil. Most issues are simple — a quick diagnosis avoids unnecessary returns.
⚠️Never process a refund or exchange without manager approval unless you've been explicitly authorised to do so. Protect yourself — get sign-off first.

Scenario practice

These are common real-world conversations you'll face. Read the scenario and the suggested response.

Sales flow
A customer walks in and says "I smoke about a pack a day and I want to try quitting. I don't know anything about vaping."
Suggested approach
"Good on you for making the switch — we'll get you set up properly. For someone coming from cigarettes, I usually recommend starting with a pod device and a nic salt juice at 20mg — it absorbs the quickest and most people find it feels closest to a cigarette. Want me to show you what we have? We can keep it simple."
Objection
A customer picks up a refillable device, looks at the price, and says "That's more than I wanted to spend."
Suggested approach
"Totally fair — it does cost more upfront. The reason most regular vapers end up here is that the juice and coils are much cheaper long-term than disposables or pods. But if you'd rather start smaller, I can show you something more entry-level — it's still a good device, just a bit more basic."
Objection
A customer returns with a device they bought last week. "The taste is really burnt — this thing's broken."
Suggested approach
"Sorry to hear that — let's have a look. Burnt taste is usually one of two things: either the coil needs replacing, or it wasn't primed before the first use. Can I ask — when you first used it, did you let it sit with juice in it for a few minutes before vaping? [If no:] That's likely what's happened — the coil's burned out. I can show you how to replace it and get you sorted."
Difficult customer
A customer is visibly frustrated and says "I come in here all the time and this place never has what I need."
Suggested approach
"I'm really sorry about that — that's genuinely frustrating. Can you tell me what you've been trying to get? I want to see if there's something we can do, or at least flag it to our manager so we can make sure we stock it going forward."
