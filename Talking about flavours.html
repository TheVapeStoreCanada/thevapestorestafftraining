Talking About Flavour

Language that goes beyond "sweet" — so you can actually sell what's in the bottle.
Why It Matters

One-word descriptions lose sales

When a customer asks what something tastes like, a vague answer ends the conversation. Specific, sensory language builds trust, sets expectations, and helps customers find what they're actually looking for — and come back for it.

Weak
"It's sweet. Kind of fruity. You'd probably like it."
Tells the customer nothing. Could describe 300 juices. No mental image forms. No trust built. They'll pick randomly or leave.
Strong
"It opens with a bright, almost tart mango — then mid-vape you get this creamy, ripe undertone, like the fruit's been left in the sun. The exhale is clean with just a little lingering sweetness."
Customer can picture it. They know what to expect. They feel like they're talking to someone who really knows the product.

Word Upgrades

Swap these tired words out

These words show up in every description and say almost nothing. Here's what to say instead.

sweet
↓
What kind? Try: sugary, jammy, honeyed, syrupy, caramelized, candy-like, ripe, brown-sugar
fruity
↓
Name the fruit, then describe it: "a ripe, jammy mango" vs. "a bright, tart citrus hit"
smooth
↓
What makes it smooth? Silky on the draw, no harshness on the throat, velvety exhale, no sharp edges
refreshing
↓
Crisp and cooling, light on the vapour, clean exhale — the menthol gives it a real brisk, almost icy finish
strong
↓
Strong flavour or throat hit? "Very bold, hits immediately" vs. "pronounced throat hit, good for higher nic"
nice / good
↓
Tell them why: "Customers come back to this one a lot — it doesn't get boring even as an all-day vape."
a bit of everything
↓
"It's layered — citrus up front, creamy mid-note, warm and sweet finish. It actually changes as you vape it."
you'll probably like it
↓
"Based on what you said, I think this one will land for you — here's why…"
Real Conversations

What to actually say

Highlighted words show where specific flavour language makes the biggest difference. Borrow the patterns, not the exact words.

Customer: "I want something sweet but not too sweet"
Don't ask "do you like fruit?" — too broad. Narrow it with one question.
"When you say too sweet, do you mean like candy-sweet, or more like heavy dessert? Because I've got a sun-warmed peach that's sweet but still feels fresh and light — versus something like [X] which is more thick and syrupy. Depends which direction you want to go."
Customer is switching from cigarettes
They want familiarity. Don't make it feel like a science experiment.
"If you're coming from cigarettes, a mellow tobacco is a natural landing spot — earthy, dry, smooth, nothing harsh. Some people also go straight to a light menthol because of that cooling throat sensation that feels familiar. Want to try both and see which your throat prefers?"
Customer: "I don't really know what I want"
Don't hand them a menu. Ask one question to split the direction.
"Do you want something that tastes like fruit, or something that tastes like food? Fruit goes from crisp and citrusy to deep and jammy. Food is more like warm, creamy, bakery stuff. Which sounds better right now?"
Customer: "I want something different from what I have"
Find out what they have and why it's not working before recommending anything.
"What's not working about it — is it too one-note, are you just bored of it, or is something about the vapour feel bugging you? Those have different fixes and I don't want to put you in something with the same problem."
Describing a complex or layered juice
Don't just list the name ingredients. Walk them through it like an experience.
"On the inhale you get this bright, almost tart green apple — very crisp. Mid-vape it shifts into something creamier and warmer, like the apple's been baked. And the exhale is clean but has this lingering caramel thing. It changes as you go — not just one flat note."
