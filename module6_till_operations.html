Till basics

The till is one of your core responsibilities on the floor. Knowing how to open it, process transactions cleanly, and close it out correctly protects both you and the store.

The rules that never change

01
Never share a till with another staff member without a handover count
If you're taking over a till mid-shift, always count it first and confirm the amount with the outgoing staff member. Both of you are responsible for what's in the drawer during your respective times — a handover count protects everyone.
02
Never open the till without a transaction
The till drawer should only open during a legitimate sale, refund, or authorised count. Opening it for any other reason — including to make change for someone who hasn't made a purchase — requires manager approval.
03
Never leave the till open or unattended
Close the drawer after every transaction. An open till is a theft risk and an invitation for errors. If you need to step away from the counter, the drawer must be closed and the screen locked.
04
Report every discrepancy — no matter how small
If the till is over or short at the end of your count, report it to your manager. Do not adjust the count to make it balance. Small discrepancies happen — unexplained or hidden ones are a serious problem.
Opening
How to open the till at the start of your shift
›
1
Receive the float from your manager or the previous shift
The opening float is a set amount of cash pre-loaded into the till at the start of trading. Your manager will confirm the expected float amount — ask if you don't know what it should be.
2
Count the float before you start serving
Count every denomination in the drawer and total them up. Do this privately — not in front of customers. If the total matches the expected float amount, you're good to go. If it doesn't, stop and notify your manager before any transactions take place.
3
Note the opening amount and sign off if required
Some stores require staff to sign a till sheet or log the opening balance in the POS system. Ask your manager what the store's procedure is. Even if no formal sign-off is required, you should know the opening float amount for your own protection.
4
Organise the drawer before the first transaction
Notes face the same direction, in the correct slots by denomination. Coins in their correct sections. A tidy drawer means faster, more accurate change-giving all shift.
💡If you're ever handed a till that hasn't been counted in front of you and something comes up short later, it becomes your responsibility. Always count before you accept it — no matter how busy it is.
Transactions
Processing sales — cash, card, and receipts

Closing
How to close down the till at the end of your shift
›
1
Process the final transaction of the shift before counting
Make sure no transactions are left pending. The till should be in a clean state before you begin the count.
2
Count all cash in the drawer by denomination
Count every denomination separately and note the totals. Count twice if the amount is large or if something doesn't add up first time. Do this away from customers.
3
Subtract the opening float to get net cash taken
Total cash in drawer minus the opening float equals the cash taken during your shift. This figure should match the sales total on the POS system for cash transactions.
4
Record and report — over or short, always tell your manager
Complete whatever till reconciliation form or POS process the store uses. If there is a discrepancy, report it immediately. Do not attempt to balance the count yourself.
5
Secure the cash as instructed
Follow your manager's instructions for where cash goes after counting — whether that's a safe, a drop box, or held for handover. Never leave counted cash sitting on the counter.
Cash handling

Accurate cash handling protects you, your colleagues, and the store. Most errors are preventable — they come from rushing, distraction, or skipping steps.

Giving change
How to count back change correctly
›

Counting change back to the customer — rather than just handing over a lump of coins — reduces errors, builds trust, and immediately resolves any dispute about the amount before it starts.

1
State the total and what the customer handed you
"That's $14.99, and you've given me $20." This creates a clear, shared reference point before anything goes into the drawer.
2
Keep the tendered note on the ledge — not in the drawer
Do not put the cash into the drawer until change has been given and accepted. If the customer disputes the amount tendered, the note is still visible.
3
Count the change out loud as you hand it over
"And that's one cent, $0.05, $0.25... and $5.00 makes $20." Counting back eliminates most disputes on the spot and takes only a few extra seconds.
4
Place the note in the drawer only after change is accepted
Once the customer has their change and is satisfied, place the tendered note into the correct denomination slot. Close the drawer.
💡If a customer hands you a large note (e.g. $100) and you're not sure you have enough change, check before you take it: "Let me just check I can break that for you." It's better than opening the drawer and having to send them elsewhere.

Counterfeit
Spotting and handling counterfeit notes
›

Canadian polymer banknotes have multiple built-in security features. Knowing them takes 30 seconds — and it protects the store from a loss that comes directly off the bottom line.

Security featureWhat to check
Transparent windowAll notes $10 and above have a clear window with a metallic portrait. It should be part of the note — not stuck on or printed on.
Holographic stripeThe metallic stripe changes colour when tilted. It should shift — a flat metallic strip that doesn't change is a red flag.
Raised inkRun your fingernail across the large number on the front — you should feel ridges. A smooth note is suspicious.
UV featuresUnder UV light, specific areas fluoresce. If your store has a UV pen or light, use it on $50 and $100 notes.
Feel of the notePolymer notes have a distinctive plastic feel — they're not paper. A note that feels like paper or is visibly flimsy should be treated with suspicion.
⚠️If you suspect a note is counterfeit: Do not return it to the customer. Do not accuse them directly. Calmly say you need to check it and call your manager immediately. Your manager will handle it from there. Do not put a suspected counterfeit into the till.

Common cash scenarios

Correct process
A customer pays $16.50 with a $20 bill. How do you process it?
Correct approach
"That's $16.50 — and you've given me $20." Place the $20 on the ledge. Take $3.50 in change from the drawer. Count it back: "25 cents, 50 cents, 75 cents — $17, $18, $19, $20. There's your $3.50 change." Hand it over, confirm they're happy, then place the $20 in the drawer and close it.
Judgment call
A customer insists they gave you a $50 but you're certain it was a $20 and you've already put it in the drawer.
Correct approach
"I understand — let me get my manager to help sort this out." Do not open the drawer and count it yourself. This is a till discrepancy situation and requires manager involvement. The manager can authorise a till check with both of you present.
Counterfeit concern
You receive a $100 note and the transparent window feels like it's been printed on rather than part of the note.
Correct approach
"Just one moment — let me grab my manager." Do not return the note. Do not accuse the customer. Get your manager immediately. Place the note aside and do not put it in the till.
Discrepancies & errors

Till discrepancies happen. How you respond to them is what matters. Always report — always be honest — never attempt to fix it yourself.

Absolute rules — no exceptions

🚫 Never adjust a count to make it balance
If your till is $5 short, do not add $5 of your own money to cover it. If it's $5 over, do not pocket it. Both are serious issues regardless of intent. Report the discrepancy as it is.
🚫 Never process a void or refund without manager approval
Even if you made the mistake — even if the customer is getting frustrated — stop the transaction and call your manager. An unauthorised void creates a discrepancy that is harder to explain than the original error.
🚫 Never accept cash for a transaction that's already been processed as card
If a customer's card was charged and they then offer cash "to make it easier," do not accept it. The transaction is complete. Mixing payment methods on a completed sale creates an impossible-to-reconcile discrepancy.

Reporting
How to report a discrepancy correctly
›

Finding a discrepancy is not automatically a problem — what matters is how it's handled. A reported discrepancy is a manageable situation. An unreported or hidden one is not.

1
Recount before reporting
Count the till a second time before you raise it. Miscounts happen. If the second count matches, report the discrepancy. If the second count gives a different number, count a third time and take the majority result.
2
Report the exact figure to your manager
Be specific: "The till is $3.75 short" or "The till is $10 over." Your manager needs the exact figure, not a rough estimate. Give them the total in the drawer and the expected total.
3
Note anything unusual from your shift
Think back through your transactions. Did anything unusual happen — a large note, a disputed change, an interrupted transaction? Giving your manager context helps them investigate.
4
Let your manager handle the investigation
Once you've reported it and given context, it's in your manager's hands. Do not attempt to investigate it yourself, do not approach a customer about a suspected error without manager direction, and do not discuss it with other staff on the floor.
💡A consistent pattern of small discrepancies is more concerning than a single larger one. If your till is frequently a few dollars off, your manager needs to know — it usually points to a specific habit or process that can be corrected.

Discrepancy scenarios

Short till
You count down at the end of your shift and the till is $8 short. You counted it twice and get the same result.
Correct approach
Report it to your manager immediately with the exact figure. Think back through your shift for anything unusual — a large note change, a transaction that felt off. Do not attempt to cover it. Being $8 short is a manageable situation. Hiding it is not.
Over till
Your closing count is $15 over the expected amount.
Correct approach
Report it exactly as you would a short. An over-till is not a windfall — it means either a counting error, a transaction error, or someone received less change than they were owed. It requires the same investigation as a short.
Transaction error
You accidentally charged a customer $46.99 instead of $16.99 on their card. The customer is still at the counter.
Correct approach
"I'm so sorry — I've made an error on that charge. Let me get my manager to sort this out for you right now." Do not attempt to void or reprocess the transaction yourself. Get your manager immediately while the customer is still there.
